80sRB (80's aRe Back) - The Project

In the 80's decade of the last century, technology revolutionized musician's behavior and the available means to create sounds.  What was before only available in the form of a wall of circuits to be patched with dozens of cords, became instantly playable thru keyboards equipped with small digital chips and, for the time, huge memories.  Moreover, MIDI, a new protocol, granted communications among practically all the products of all manufacturers. These two miracles paved the path that led to the creation of the music that we all remember and love.

Today's capabilities of WebAudioAPI are promising a somehow comparable revolution, giving musicians the power to create music within nominally any browser. So we set our sight to investigate if it would have been feasible to harvest such a web ambient to re-create a musical instrument, geared with synthesizing capabilities, and with MIDI.  

We wanted a programmable analog subtractive synthesizer with two oscillators, a decent filter, and capable to answer to the very basic MIDI messages like notes on/off and Pitch Bend.  Most of all, we wanted it to sound properly and to be nothing less than a real Musical Instrument. We feel we've made it and we are happy to present you, here, the 80sRB.

Along the way, we have discovered that there are many possibilities, but also quite a few obstacles and, as expected, new limitations that will possibly need to be addressed within the protocol itself.  It has to be said, though, that few limits from the past are already solved.  That's why, here, you will find a "80's Limited" page, where all the results we obtained are presented under the limitations of the first keyboards from the beginning of that decade.  Namely, no MIDI Velocity and only four notes (8 oscillators) of polyphony.

In order to obtain musically useful results, WebAudioAPI, at the moment, in our opinion, needs to be 'bent' and 'supervised'.   We have spent a lot of efforts to achieve 'musicality' from functions that are more designed to be useful for code writers.  Ramping curves have only two possibilities: linear and exponential, while there's no 'inverse exponential' counterpart.  Time handling for destroying oscillators could lead to pops and the way MIDI protocol is handled is not as straightforward as it should be expected... Still, we have done our best but, as it always happens with software, the door is open for future improvements.
